Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Selected Dataset: Climate Change Indicators

Proposition: “Global warming and climate change is causing a significant rise in sea levels around the world”.

Visualization 1: Supporting Proposition:

  • Usage of Area Graph
    • Score: 2
    • Employed an area graph to depict the progression of sea levels over time, leveraging its ability to visually demonstrate the continuous rise in sea levels. The choice of an area graph effectively showcases the cumulative increase in sea levels, emphasizing the growing volume of water. While alternatives like bar or line graphs were considered, the area graph was selected for its capacity to highlight the sustained rise in sea levels and facilitate the visualization of temporal growth.
  • Extrapolation
    • Score: -1.5
    • Incorporated an extrapolated segment at the graph's conclusion to underscore the potential escalation of sea levels. Opted for a particularly dramatic extrapolation to accentuate the impact and bolster the proposition, enhancing the graph's visual impact. The decision to conclude the graph at 2020 rather than 2023 was motivated by a slight decline in sea levels observed between 2020 and 2023, focusing solely on the period characterized by a steep upward trend.
  • Heatmap Color
    • Score: 0.5
    • Utilized a heatmap color scheme to accentuate the visual representation of sea level growth. The color scheme transitions from deep blue to yellow, symbolizing higher sea levels with "hotter" colors. This alignment with the concept of global warming visually underscores the severity and significance of the sea level rise.
  • Annotations of Sunken Islands
    • Score: -2
    • Included annotations of submerged islands with downward-pointing arrows, visually indicating their submergence coinciding with sea level rise. This tactic evokes an emotional response from viewers by highlighting the displacement of communities and homes, despite potential factors beyond rising sea levels contributing to their submergence.
  • Axis fills the page
    • Score: 2
    • Employed an axis that fills the page and accommodates the data effectively to accurately portray the increasing sea levels over time. Supplementary annotations denoting peak sea level increases at the end of the dataset and extrapolation further emphasize the upward trajectory of sea level rise.

    Visualization 2: Against Proposition:

  • Flipped Axis
    • Score: -2
    • Inverted the axis to present the graph as if illustrating land area relative to sea level, giving the impression that higher sea levels result from lower "land" areas accumulating more water. This manipulation creates the illusion that sea levels are not significantly rising, as the apparent increase in water level is masked by the "land" descending. This deceptive tactic leads viewers to perceive minimal sea level rise.
  • Large y axis range
    • Score: -1.5
    • Expanded the y-axis range to compress the data towards the center, minimizing steep peaks and valleys. This adjustment makes the data appear less pronounced and less indicative of a substantial increase or decrease, thereby diminishing the perceived magnitude of sea level rise.
  • Bar graph
    • Score: 0.5
    • Opted for a bar graph instead of a line graph or area graph to obscure the overall trend of sea level increase over time. The bar graph introduces additional complexity to the representation of "land" and aligns with the chosen metaphor. This deliberate choice complicates initial interpretation, making the graph slightly more challenging to decipher at first glance.
  • Pale color scheme
    • Score: 0.5
    • Selected a pale color scheme reflecting a metaphorical landscape where light brown represents land, blue represents water, and light blue signifies the sky. This choice imparts a lighthearted mood to the graph, trivializing the issue by employing pastel colors that detract from the gravity of the content.
  • Only plotting Bering Sea
    • Score: 1
    • Limited the dataset to the Bering Sea instead of global data, as it exhibited a relatively flat trend line with minimal sea level rise. By exclusively plotting this data, the intent is to persuade viewers that sea levels are not escalating significantly worldwide, as evidenced by the lack of substantial change in this particular location. This selective presentation enhances the argument against the proposition by highlighting a location where sea level rise appears subdued.

    Final Reflection

    In designing the two visualizations supporting and opposing the proposition regarding sea level rise, I encountered both straightforward aspects and challenges. The straightforward elements included selecting appropriate graph types and color schemes to effectively convey the intended message. However, one of the primary difficulties arose in ethically navigating the line between persuasive visualization techniques and potentially misleading representations. Crafting the visualizations required careful consideration of how to present data in a manner that accurately reflected the underlying trends while still aligning with the intended persuasive goals.

    What surprised me most was the subtle yet significant impact that small design choices could have on the perceived message of the visualizations. For instance, the use of different graph types, color schemes, and annotations could drastically influence viewers' interpretations of the data. This realization underscored the importance of ethical analysis and visualization in ensuring that visualizations accurately represent the data without misleading or manipulating viewers.

    Ethical analysis and visualization, to me, now entail a thorough examination of not only the data itself but also the design choices made in presenting that data. It involves considering the potential implications of those choices on viewers' perceptions and understanding of the subject matter. Ethical visualization involves transparency, honesty, and integrity in representing data accurately and responsibly, while still allowing for persuasive communication within ethical bounds.

    Drawing bounds to distinguish between acceptable persuasive choices and misleading ones can be challenging. However, I believe that transparency and context are key factors in making this determination. Acceptable persuasive choices involve transparently presenting data in a manner that enhances understanding and promotes informed decision-making. Misleading choices, on the other hand, involve intentionally distorting or misrepresenting data to manipulate viewers' perceptions. While there may not always be clear-cut boundaries, a commitment to transparency, accuracy, and ethical responsibility can guide the development of persuasive visualizations that uphold integrity and trustworthiness.
